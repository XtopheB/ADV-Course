Why this matters: colour, iconography, wording and even ordering are all data too. A palette that maps "women" to pink and "men" to blue, icons that show women only with children and men only with briefcases, a headline that treats one sex as the norm and the other as the exception, or always listing one category first — all assert something the underlying indicator never claimed. Note also that filling a human silhouette to represent a share (as in the reference, left) is itself a design choice worth discussing: it can make a statistic feel more human, but it also risks presenting the body itself as the unit of measurement. A neutral, colourblind-safe, legible design lets the numbers make the argument instead of the imagery.
